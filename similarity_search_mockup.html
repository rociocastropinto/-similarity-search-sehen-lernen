What Is Similarity Search?

🎯 Learning Goal

The Core Idea

Imagine browsing 10,000 historical paintings trying to find all works similar to a specific altarpiece. Manually, that could take weeks. Similarity Search automates this by translating visual content into mathematics: each image becomes a long sequence of numbers (a feature vector), and similarity is measured by how close those number sequences are.


This is not keyword search or metadata matching — the system looks directly at the pixels: shapes, textures, colours, structures. It can find connections that human cataloguers might never notice.

Two Search Modes on the Platform

🔍
Single vs All
Choose one query image → find all similar images in your dataset, ranked by score.
⚖️
Pairwise
Choose exactly two images → get a precise similarity score comparing them directly.

The 5 Feature Methods

Each method "sees" images differently. Click a card to learn more about each approach.

🧠
CNN (Convolutional Neural Network)
General Purpose
Deep learning layers detect edges → shapes → complex objects. Best default choice for most image types. Mimics how the human visual cortex works.
Use when: you want a general-purpose comparison without a specific hypothesis.
📐
HOG (Histogram of Oriented Gradients)
Shape & Outline
Analyses direction and strength of edges in image regions. Captures how brightness changes across the image — ideal for shapes and contours.
Use when: figural compositions, architectural forms, or silhouettes matter.
🔑
SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature Transform)
Scale & Rotation
Finds distinctive "keypoints" — stable landmarks recognisable even if the image is rotated, scaled, or viewed from different angles.
Use when: images may be cropped, rotated, or exist in different reproduction formats.
🎨
Histogram
Color Palette
Compares colour distribution patterns regardless of spatial arrangement. Images with similar palettes score as similar.
Use when: colour scheme is your primary research interest. Caution: ignores composition.
📜
Manuscript (Specialized)
Historical Documents
Custom algorithm for historical documents. Combines: CLAHE (contrast enhancement for faded texts) + Multi-scale HOG (Histogram of Oriented Gradients) (fine details & coarse layout) + Layout analysis (text line and word structure).
Use when: working with manuscripts, incunabula, charters, papyri, or any handwritten material.
⚡ Quick Decision Guide

General images: CNN (Convolutional Neural Network) · Shapes/figures: HOG (Histogram of Oriented Gradients) · Rotated/scaled: SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature Transform) · Colour palette: Histogram · Manuscripts: Manuscript

Distance Metrics

MODULE 03
🎯 Learning Goal

The Three Distance Metrics

Select a metric to see how it interprets similarity:

📐 Cosine
📏 Euclidean
🏙️ Manhattan
Cosine Distance

Measures the angle between two feature vectors. If they point in exactly the same direction, distance = 0 (identical). Ignores overall brightness/scale — focuses on patterns. Recommended default.

Score interpretation (Cosine):
0.0–0.1 · Highly similar
0.05
0.1–0.3 · Strongly similar
0.22
0.3–0.5 · Moderately similar
0.38
0.5–1.0 · Weak/no similarity
0.70
⚠️ Important Caveat

Similarity scores are always relative to your dataset. A score of 0.3 may be very significant in a diverse collection but unremarkable in a homogeneous one. Always examine results visually alongside the numbers.
